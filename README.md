# School Shootings Were Most Common at High Schools (2009–2018)
## My dataset was downloaded from Data Is Plural, but the original source is the K–12 School Shooting Database, which is maintained by researchers at the Naval Postgraduate School, an academic institution. The database documents incidents in which a gun was fired, brandished, or a bullet struck school property between 1970 and the present. The version I analyzed contains 180 school shootings from 2009–2018, focusing only on incidents where at least one person was shot.Because the data comes from an academic source with a clearly defined methodology, it is generally trustworthy. However, I would still verify the information by comparing it with reports from law enforcement, local news organizations, or other databases such as those maintained by CNN or The Washington Post. No dataset should be accepted as completely accurate without fact-checking. One challenge is that the way a "school shooting" is defined can vary between organizations. This database includes any incident in which at least one person was shot on school property during the selected time period, while other organizations may use different definitions or include different types of incidents. The dataset also only covers the years 2009–2018, so it does not represent more recent trends. In addition, the data may not capture every factor that contributed to each incident, such as mental health history, school security measures, or community conditions. These limitations are important to consider when interpreting the results, since the data provides useful information but does not tell the complete story.
## My first pivot table counted the number of incidents by state. California had the highest number of recorded incidents with 19, followed by Florida (14), Texas (12), and North Carolina (10). Several other states, including Michigan, Illinois, Georgia, and Alabama, also had relatively high totals, while many states had only one or two recorded incidents. This shows that the incidents in this dataset were not distributed evenly across the United States. However, I found it interesting that the states with the highest counts are also among the country's most populous states. Because this pivot table only reports raw counts, it does not account for differences in population or the number of schools in each state. As a result, these numbers should not be interpreted as indicating that these states are necessarily more dangerous than others.
## My second pivot table grouped incidents by school type. High schools accounted for 118 of the 180 incidents, making them by far the most common setting in the dataset. Elementary schools had 30 incidents, and middle schools had 24, while K–12 schools and other grade configurations each represented only a small number of cases. I found this result interesting because it suggests that school shootings in this dataset occurred much more frequently at high schools than at elementary or middle schools. One possible explanation is that high schools generally have older students, larger student populations, and different social environments. ## This project analyzed a dataset of 180 school shootings that occurred between 2009 and 2018. After organizing the data in Google Sheets, I created pivot tables and visualizations to better understand patterns by state and school type. My analysis showed that California had the highest number of recorded incidents in the dataset, followed by Florida and Texas. I also found that high schools accounted for the majority of incidents, with 118 of the 180 shootings occurring at high schools. These visualizations made it much easier to identify trends than simply reading through individual records. Although the dataset comes from an academic source and is widely used for research, it still has limitations. The data only includes incidents from 2009 through 2018, so it does not reflect more recent events. In addition, the dataset reports raw counts of incidents rather than rates based on the number of schools or the population in each state. For example, California has the largest number of recorded incidents, but it also has one of the largest populations and the greatest number of schools. Without considering those factors, the results could be misleading. There are also important ethical concerns when reporting on school shootings. Simply ranking states or school types could unintentionally stigmatize certain communities or create the impression that one state or type of school is inherently more dangerous than another. Journalists should provide context, explain the limitations of the data, and avoid sensationalizing these tragedies. Every incident represents real people, families, and communities who were affected, so the information should be presented respectfully and accurately. If I were reporting this story as a journalist, I would include additional information such as population size, the number of schools in each state, and whether school shooting rates have changed over time. I would also compare this dataset with other reliable sources to verify the findings and ensure the data is complete. By combining the numbers with additional context, the story would provide a more accurate and balanced understanding of school shootings rather than relying only on raw counts.
